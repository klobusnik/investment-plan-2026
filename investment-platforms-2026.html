Investing from the Czech Republic — Platform Guide

A from-scratch guide to ETF investing platforms available to a Czech resident, with current 2025/2026 fees, a beginner glossary, the Czech tax rules that actually matter, and where to park money for just 1–2 years. Built for someone earning CZK with little prior knowledge of investing.

Start here — the 6 things that matter most

If you read nothing else, read this. The rest of the page backs up each point.

1. Fees are the enemy you control

You can't control markets, but you control fees. The gap between a 0.2% ETF and a 2% advisor product can quietly eat a third to a half of your final pot over decades. Play with the fee calculator.

2. Buy Irish (IE00…) accumulating ETFs

An accumulating, Ireland-domiciled UCITS ETF (ISIN starts IE00) reinvests dividends automatically — no yearly dividend to declare. Held 3+ years the gain is tax-free. That's near-zero tax and near-zero paperwork.

3. The 3-year time test is your superpower

Hold securities ≥ 3 years → capital gain is exempt from Czech income tax. From 2026 there's no upper limit for securities. This single rule shapes everything below.

4. Use DIP only for money you'll lock to age 60

DIP deducts up to 48,000 CZK/yr from your tax base (saves ~7,200 CZK), but it's locked until age 60 AND 10 years. Great for a slice; the rest goes in a normal account where the 3-year rule already makes it tax-free and flexible.

5. Short-term money ≠ stocks

Need the cash in 1–2 years? Stocks can drop 30% at the worst moment, and a sale under 3 years is taxable. Use a savings account, term deposit, or the tax-free state bond instead. See short-term.

6. Watch the hidden FX fee

ETFs trade in USD/EUR, so your CZK gets converted — and the platform skims a spread each way. At 500k/yr this matters: it ranges from 0.002% (IBKR) to 1.5–2% (Patria, Revolut over limit).

Concepts, explained from zero

Plain-English definitions with tiny examples. Open the ones you need.

Funds & ETFs

What is an ETF?
A single thing you buy like a share on an exchange that holds hundreds or thousands of companies at once. Buy one ETF and you instantly own a tiny slice of everything inside it — no stock-picking needed.
Buy one share of a "world" ETF for ~3,000 CZK → you own a sliver of Apple, Microsoft, Nestlé and ~1,500 other firms.
Index fund vs active fund
An index fund just copies a published list (an index) — no manager picking stocks, so fees are tiny. An active fund pays a manager to try to beat the market. Over the long run, low-cost index funds beat most active funds after fees.
Accumulating vs distributing (important for Czechs)
Distributing pays dividends to you as cash. Accumulating reinvests them inside the fund automatically. For Czech investors accumulating is usually better: no dividend payout means nothing to declare or tax each year — you only deal with tax when you sell, and after 3 years that sale is tax-free.
TER (expense ratio)
The fund's yearly running cost, taken automatically from its value (you never get a bill). Broad index ETF ≈ 0.07–0.22%/yr; active bank fund ≈ 1.5–2.5%.
0.20% TER on 500,000 CZK = ~1,000 CZK/year, quietly deducted.
S&P 500 vs MSCI World vs FTSE All-World
These are indexes (the lists ETFs copy). S&P 500 = 500 biggest US firms (US-only, tech-heavy). MSCI World = ~1,400 firms across 23 developed countries (no emerging markets). FTSE All-World = ~3,700 firms, developed + emerging — the broadest one-fund choice.
Where you buy: broker vs robo vs bank fund vs advisor
Broker (XTB, IBKR, DEGIRO, Fio): you pick and buy yourself — cheapest. Robo-advisor (Portu, Fondee): answer a questionnaire, it builds & auto-manages a portfolio — convenient, ~0.5–1%/yr. Bank fund: easy to start but usually worst value (high TER + entry fee). Advisor-led (Edward, Partners): personal advice but often big up-front + ongoing fees — read the fee sheet carefully.

Fees & how they eat returns

The fee types, one line each
Entry / front-load: one-time cut when you pay in (3% on 500k = 15,000 CZK gone). Annual management: yearly % a robo/manager charges. Fund TER: the fund's own internal cost (stacks on top of management). FX / conversion: spread taken converting CZK↔USD/EUR, each way. Exit: cut when you sell. Per-trade commission: fixed/% per order — hurts small frequent buys. Inactivity: charged if you don't trade. Custody: small yearly fee just to hold assets. Spread: invisible gap between buy/sell price.
Why 1% vs 0.2% is a huge deal
A fee doesn't cost you "1%" — it compounds against you every year. Over 30 years an extra ~0.8%/yr quietly eats roughly a fifth of your final pot. This is why TER is the single most important number to minimise. The calculator above shows it live.

Growth, risk & behaviour

Compounding
Your returns start earning their own returns. 500,000 CZK at 7% → 535,000 next year; then 7% is earned on 535,000, not 500,000. A small head-start snowballs enormously over decades.
Expected return & volatility
Expected return is a realistic long-run average (~6–8%/yr nominal for global stocks), not a promise — single years swing from −40% to +30%. Volatility = how violently the value bounces. Stocks are volatile; savings accounts are not.
Volatility drag
Losses hurt more than equal gains help (−50% needs +100% to recover). So bumpy returns compound to less than the simple average. Rough rule: real growth ≈ average − (volatility² ÷ 2).
Risk vs time horizon & diversification
The longer you can leave money untouched, the more stock risk you can take — you can ride out crashes. Money needed in 1–2 years should take almost no risk. Diversification (a broad ETF spreads across thousands of firms) means one bankruptcy barely dents you.
Regular investing (DCA) vs lump sum
Dollar-cost averaging = invest a fixed amount monthly, smoothing the price you pay and removing "did I buy at the top?" stress. Lump sum = invest it all at once; statistically wins more often (markets rise more than fall). With 500,000 CZK/year, ~42,000 CZK/month is a sensible default.

Inflation, currency & dividends

Real vs nominal return (inflation)
Nominal = the headline number; real = after inflation (actual purchasing power). Czech inflation peaked >15% in 2022–23 and has since cooled toward ČNB's ~2% target. 6% nominal − 2.5% inflation ≈ 3.5% real. Cash earning less than inflation is silently losing value.
Currency risk & FX drift for a CZK investor
Your salary is CZK but a world ETF holds USD/EUR assets. If CZK strengthens, your CZK value drops even if the ETF rose in dollars. FX drift is the slow long-term trend (if CZK tends to strengthen, that's a mild headwind). Over decades it mostly washes out but adds short-term wobble.
CZK-hedged ETFs
Versions that cancel out currency moves so you get the market return without the CZK/USD wobble. They cost a bit more (higher TER) and the hedge isn't free; most long-term investors skip hedging on equities and accept the noise.
Dividend tax & W-8BEN
The US withholds 15% on dividends to foreigners — but 30% if you haven't filed a W-8BEN form. Crucially, with an Irish ETF the fund handles this for you (only 15% internally), so you usually don't file W-8BEN yourself.
Fund domicile: Irish (IE00) vs US — prefer Irish
Where the fund is registered. Irish UCITS (ISIN IE00…) beats US-domiciled for Czechs: only 15% internal US dividend tax (vs 30%), accumulating versions need no yearly declaration, and no US estate-tax exposure above ~$60k. Most US-domiciled ETFs aren't even legally sellable to EU retail anyway. Rule of thumb: prefer IE00.
Liquidity & the emergency fund
Liquidity = how fast you can turn it into cash without loss. Keep an emergency fund of ~3–6 months' expenses in instant-access cash before investing. Never put money you'll need within ~2–3 years into stocks.

Czech tax & DIP — what actually matters

Verified for the 2025/2026 rules (Ministry of Finance, ČNB). This is general information, not tax advice — confirm your own case.

⏳ Time test — 3 years

0% tax

Hold securities (shares, ETFs, fund units) ≥ 3 years → capital gain is exempt from income tax. (Company ownership stakes: 5 years.) The cornerstone of tax-efficient investing. Still in force 2026.

💯 Value test — 100,000 CZK

≤ 100k

If your total gross proceeds from selling securities in a year are ≤ 100,000 CZK, it's exempt regardless of holding period. Note: gross sale proceeds, not profit. Threshold unchanged for 2025/26.

🔝 The 40M cap

gone (2026)

A 40,000,000 CZK/yr cap on the time-test exemption was added in 2025, then removed for securities from 1 Jan 2026 (kept only for crypto). Irrelevant unless you sell >40M in a year.

📊 Tax rates

15% / 23%

Taxable gains and dividends are added to your income: 15% up to ~1.68M CZK total, 23% above. Most investors pay 15% on anything taxable.

DIP — Dlouhodobý investiční produkt (the retirement wrapper)

How the DIP tax break works
Deduct your own DIP contributions from your tax base up to 48,000 CZK/year → saves up to 7,200 CZK (15% × 48,000). This 48k cap is shared across DIP + pension (penzijko) + life insurance — it's one combined ceiling, not per product.
Employer contributions
An employer can add up to 50,000 CZK/year free of income tax and social/health insurance (also a shared limit across the three old-age products). Employer money does not eat into your own 48k personal deduction.
The lock-up — read before signing
To keep the benefit you must hold at least 10 years AND withdraw no earlier than the year you turn 60 — both conditions. Break it (withdraw even 1 CZK early) and you must repay all the tax relief claimed over the last 10 years.
What qualifies & who offers it
Broad: shares, ETFs, bonds, fund units, and cash held inside the DIP. Offered by banks, brokers and investment companies — incl. Patria (best-rated), Portu, Fondee, Fio, Conseq, Česká spořitelna, MONETA. Among low-cost foreign DIY brokers: XTB yes; Trading 212, DEGIRO, IBKR, eToro, Revolut: no.
Practical advice for a 500k/yr investor
Only ~48k of your annual contribution gets the deduction, and DIP money is locked to age 60. Use DIP for the slice you're certain you won't touch before retirement; invest the rest in a normal brokerage account, where the 3-year time test already makes gains tax-free with full flexibility.
Reporting foreign ETF gains/dividends (DIY)
No automatic Czech withholding on a foreign broker — you self-report. Capital gains: §10 (nothing owed if time/value test passes). Dividends: §8 gross at 15%, with foreign tax often creditable via treaty. Currency: convert each transaction at the ČNB rate on the trade date, or use the single annual rate — pick one method and stick to it. Accumulating Irish ETFs held 3+ years often mean zero tax paperwork.

Short-term: parking ~500,000 CZK for 1–2 years

You want to put ~500k somewhere for one or two years, then take it out. Do not use stock ETFs for this. Here's why, and what to use instead. Everything below is driven by the ČNB 2-week repo rate = 3.50% (held since May 2025).

⚠️ Why equity ETFs are the wrong tool here

1. Volatility: stocks can fall 20–40% in months — a real chance you'd sell at a loss exactly when you need the cash. 2. Tax: selling under 3 years misses the time test, so the gain is taxable at 15% (your 500k easily blows past the 100k value test). Short-term equity gains are both risky and taxed.

The simple plan for 500k over 1–2 years

Split by when you'll need each chunk: the tax-free Dluhopis Republiky Flexi bond (3.5%) for the bulk you can leave 3+ months at a time, a top savings account (~4% gross / instant access) for the part you might need any day, and a 12-month term deposit (~3.5%) for money you definitely won't touch for a year. Interest from savings/deposits has 15% tax auto-deducted by the bank (no reporting); the state bond's interest is fully tax-free.

What changed since ~2023

This guide updates a plan that's about two years old. The biggest shifts for a Czech ETF investor:

2024 →

DIP went mainstream

The Dlouhodobý investiční produkt launched Jan 2024 (~116k accounts in year one). Tax deduction up to 48k/yr. Now offered by Patria, Portu, Fondee, Fio, banks — and XTB. Not by T212, DEGIRO, IBKR, eToro, Revolut.

2023–26

Interest on idle cash became a thing

XTB, Trading 212 (CZK ~3.7%) and IBKR (CZK ~1.7%) now pay interest on uninvested cash. DEGIRO and Saxo (on CZK) still pay 0% — a real reason to avoid them for any cash buffer.

Oct 2025

DEGIRO overhauled ETF fees

The old "1 free trade/ETF/month + €1" model is gone. Now a flat €1/trade on ~1,600 Tradegate ETFs, unlimited, connectivity fee removed. Non-Tradegate ETF commission rose €2→€3.

Apr 2025

Patria cut fees ~80%

New platform: US min commission $14.90 → $2.90+0.3%; EU €16.90 → €4.90+0.3%. Free recurring ETF for ČSOB clients & under-30s. Built the best-rated DIP.

Jan 2025

Revolut launched free ETF plans in CZ

Zero-commission recurring auto-invest into 300+ EU-listed UCITS ETFs from €1. The biggest new cheap-passive entrant — but no DIP, and 2% FX above the monthly limit.

Aug 2024

IBKR moved EU clients to Ireland

All EU/CZ clients migrated to Interactive Brokers Ireland. Investor protection dropped from €100,000 to €20,000 (Irish ICS) — relevant right at a 500k deposit size.

2025

Saxo trimmed fees but added a CZK trap

Min commission cut to $1, inactivity fee abolished, custody removed in core EU — except CZ, where you must opt into securities lending to reach 0% custody. CZK cash now earns 0%.

2026

Banks got cheaper for ETFs

Česká spořitelna cuts Xetra buys to 0.35% (min €4) from Feb 2026; Raiffeisen trades Prague stocks at 0.11%. The 40M time-test cap was removed for securities. Mar 2026: J. Safra Sarasin acquired ~71% of Saxo.
